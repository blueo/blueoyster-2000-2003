07 / 16.04.02
Transitional Places
Ali Bramwell

In this body of work Bramwell used predominantly steel. The industrial character of the metal was softened by the fires of the forge, and altered by chrome electroplating or powder coating. The show also featured a liberal use of barbed wire, which brings an implication of threat into the work. Bramwell believes barbed wire has an already ambiguous character for most New Zealanders... it is used so much on our farms barbed wire recalls quiet farming scenes as well as images of violence.		 The way that Bramwell used it is similarly ambiguous: the possibility of the barbs tearing your skin still repels; but there is also an eerie and alluring sensuality, a strange kind of beauty, in the work that when combined with the sense of ambiguous threat makes the experience of the work overlaid with a surreal or dreamlike quality. This exhibition was concerned with implications of fear and identity politics. Security and comfort are both necessary and potentially dangerous. Fear causes us to contract and reinforce boundaries. Threat causes us to cling to what we know. The hairs on your body raise and prickle, your skin is alive and listening.
